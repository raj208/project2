About the Santhals

The Santhal people, a prominent tribal community in India, inhabit regions across Jharkhand, West Bengal, Bihar, Odisha, and Assam. Speaking their native language Santhali, they traditionally rely on agriculture, cultivating crops like rice. The Santhal culture is rich, featuring vibrant music and dance forms such as the "Chadheya" dance. Their festivals, including Baha, Sohrai, and Mage Parab, are linked to agricultural practices. Following their indigenous religion, Santhals worship spirits, ancestors, and natural elements. Socially, they have a structured society with a village headman ("Majhi") and a council ("Majhi-Pargana"). Known for their artistic skills, Santhals engage in traditional art, craft, and weaving, depicting scenes from daily life and cultural stories. Challenges faced by the Santhal community include land rights and preserving cultural identity amid modernization. Ongoing efforts focus on education, healthcare, and cultural preservation for the Santhal people.

Identity

The word ‘Santhal’ is derived from two words; santa meaning calm and peaceful and ala meaning man. In the past, the Santhals were leading a nomadic life. Gradually they came to settle down in the Chhotanagpur plateau. Towards the end of 18th Century they migrated to the Santhal Parganas of Bihar and then they came to Odisha/West Bengal. They speak Santhali language which belongs to the Santhal group of Austro-Asiatic sub family of languages. The Santhali has its own script called “Ol chiki” invented by Pundit Raghunath Murmu.

By their physical features the Santhals have long head, brownish body colour and flat nose. The Santhals are progressive community.
